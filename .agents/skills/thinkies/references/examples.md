# Thinkies Application Examples

These examples illustrate how Thinkies apply to real product and software development scenarios. Use them to inspire or guide application, but consider what fits your specific context.

All Thinkies were created and curated by Kent Beck (https://kentbeck.com/).

---

## Example 1: Prioritization in Product Development

**Situation:**
Overwhelmed by huge backlog of features. Everyone says everything is important. Struggling to prioritize.

**Relevant Thinkies:**

**"Half in Half"**
Pattern: You receive a long scope and timeline.
Transformation: Ask, "Which half can we deliver in half the time?" This often reveals what's truly essential.
_This Thinkie was created and curated by Kent Beck (https://kentbeck.com/)._

**"Customer's Voice"**
Pattern: You're being pulled in different directions by many stakeholders.
Transformation: Unify them into a single coherent voice, so you can focus clearly.
_This Thinkie was created and curated by Kent Beck (https://kentbeck.com/)._

**"Compared to What?"**
Pattern: Someone says, "This is the most important feature."
Transformation: Ask, "Compared to what?" This clarifies priorities and relative value.
_This Thinkie was created and curated by Kent Beck (https://kentbeck.com/)._

## Example 2: Technical Debt and Refactoring

**Situation:**
Lot of technical debt. Every time team tries to fix it, feels too big and they give up.

**Relevant Thinkies:**

**"Scale Down"**
Pattern: You feel overwhelmed by a big problem.
Transformation: Ask, "What smaller thing could make the big problem more manageable?" Focus on that first.
_This Thinkie was created and curated by Kent Beck (https://kentbeck.com/)._

**"Grow Then Split"**
Pattern: You're unsure where to start when a system will eventually have many parts.
Transformation: Start small, grow it, and then split naturally as it becomes too big.
_This Thinkie was created and curated by Kent Beck (https://kentbeck.com/)._

**"Easy Bit"**
Pattern: You're paralyzed by magnitude.
Transformation: Pick the absolute easiest small improvement and start there to build momentum.
_This Thinkie was created and curated by Kent Beck (https://kentbeck.com/)._

## Example 3: Deciding Between New Product Ideas

**Situation:**
Debating between launching two new features. Both seem valuable, but can't decide which to build first.

**Relevant Thinkies:**

**"Parallel Decision"**
Pattern: You're stuck choosing between two alternatives.
Transformation: Ask, "Why not both?" Explore both until one clearly wins.
_This Thinkie was created and curated by Kent Beck (https://kentbeck.com/)._

**"Set-Based Design"**
Pattern: You don't have enough info to choose wisely.
Transformation: Implement small parts of each option until one is clearly superior.
_This Thinkie was created and curated by Kent Beck (https://kentbeck.com/)._

**"Defer Decision"**
Pattern: A decision is being made too early without enough information or support.
Transformation: Wait. Ask, "What if we delay deciding for a while?"
_This Thinkie was created and curated by Kent Beck (https://kentbeck.com/)._

## Example 4: Delivery Speed Pressures

**Situation:**
Leadership keeps saying "We need to go faster!" Team already working as hard as possible and burning out.

**Relevant Thinkies:**

**"Latency, Throughput, & Variance"**
Pattern: You're told to go faster, but it isn't clear what that really means.
Transformation: Ask if the focus is on latency or throughput. Reducing variance often improves perceived speed without overloading the team.
_This Thinkie was created and curated by Kent Beck (https://kentbeck.com/)._

**"Reduce Utilization"**
Pattern: You need to do more, but you're at maximum capacity.
Transformation: Ask, "What would happen if we intentionally reduced utilization?" Counterintuitively, it can improve flow and reduce wait times.
_This Thinkie was created and curated by Kent Beck (https://kentbeck.com/)._

**"Work/Wait Ratio"**
Pattern: Work is constantly waiting in queues.
Transformation: Trace a recent work item, add up active work time vs. waiting time, and improve the ratio.
_This Thinkie was created and curated by Kent Beck (https://kentbeck.com/)._
