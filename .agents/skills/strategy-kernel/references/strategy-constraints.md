# Strategy Constraints

Constraints are fundamental to strategy. Good strategy works around organizational limitations and leverages external dynamics to create advantage.

## Enabling Constraints

Limitations inherent to the situation or rivals that a good strategy can exploit to create leverage:

- **Scarcity and the Need for Choice** — Limited resources make strategy necessary because they force focus and choice. If resources were unlimited, there would be no need for selection. Scarcity is strategy's parent.
- **Rival Inertia and Entropy** — Rivals' inability to adapt is a source of advantage for an agile competitor. Proxy inertia — when a rival fails to respond to protect existing profit streams — can be directly exploited.
- **Isolating Mechanisms** — Barriers that prevent competitors from duplicating your advantage: patents, reputation, network effects, know-how. Strengthening these creates a constraint for rivals.
- **Chain-Link Logic** — When a system's performance is limited by its weakest link, improving other links is useless. Focus on the critical link to unblock performance. Tightly linked systems are also hard for rivals to replicate (they must match every link).
- **Coordination vs. Resources Trade-off** — Resources and tight coordination are partial substitutes. Fewer resources demand smarter, tighter integration of actions to compensate — this design coherence itself becomes competitive advantage.

## Limiting Constraints

Internal or external obstacles that must be diagnosed and either overcome or absorbed by strategy:

- **Organizational Inertia** — The unwillingness or inability to adapt to changing circumstances. Manifests as:
  - *Routine inertia:* Standard procedures and outdated rules filter information and limit action to the familiar
  - *Cultural inertia:* Deeply rooted work norms and mindsets that resist change
- **Entropy** — Organizations tend toward disorganization, loss of focus, and inefficiency over time. Constant work is required to maintain purpose and form.
- **Complexity and Ambiguity** — In ill-structured situations, complexity is overwhelming and there is no obvious list of good actions. The leader must absorb this ambiguity to define a feasible Proximate Objective.
- **Costs of Integrated Design** — Tightly integrated strategies create advantage but carry costs: harder to create, more fragile in execution, narrower in focus, less flexible when facing unforeseen changes.
- **Cognitive Limits** — The human mind has finite resources, leading to shortsightedness and premature closure of judgment. Strategy must guide attention and help overcome these biases.
